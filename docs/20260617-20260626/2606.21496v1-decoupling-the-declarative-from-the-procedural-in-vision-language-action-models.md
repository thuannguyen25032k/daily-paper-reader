## Abstract
Deploying generalist robotic agents in the real world requires transferable skills. Specifically, a policy trained to clone a behavior from object-specific demonstrations must generalize beyond that object, otherwise data collection requirements become intractable. Recently, fine-tuning of pre-trained billion-parameter Vision-Language Models (VLMs), initially on large-scale robot datasets and then on fewer scenario-specific demonstrations, has emerged as the predominant paradigm for designing Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models. While these policies achieve state-of-the-art manipulation performance in-distribution, they remain brittle to minor spatial, semantic, and task variations. In this work, we address the inability of current models to decouple the declarative (i.e., concepts and entity semantics) from the procedural knowledge (i.e., how to do something) encoded in their parameters, which is a fundamental bottleneck for zero-shot skill transfer to novel objects. To address this, we propose w$^{2}$VLA, a new VLA model with restructured information flow. Rather than feeding all multimodal tokens from the VLM encoder into a large, opaque transformer-based action expert, our approach modulates the robot state sequence with visual, spatial, and skill information in a compositional and interpretable manner. Unlike popular, state-of-the-art VLAs, we show that our modular approach successfully decouples knowledge representations, enabling robust behavior cloning and unprecedented zero-shot skill transfer capabilities across dissimilar, unseen objects.

# Summary

## 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning
Current state-of-the-art Vision‑Language‑Action (VLA) models (e.g., π0.5, OTTER) encode declarative knowledge (object identity, spatial properties, semantics) and procedural knowledge (how to execute a skill) into a single, opaque representation. This entanglement causes policies to overfit on spurious object–skill correlations seen during training. Consequently, they fail at **zero‑shot skill transfer**—applying a learned skill (e.g., “rotate 90°”) to a novel object (e.g., a different fruit). This brittleness is a fundamental bottleneck for generalist robot agents and cannot be solved purely by scaling data or model parameters.

## 2. Methodology
- **Core idea**: Decouple information flow into explicit **where** (declarative/spatial) and **what** (procedural/skill) modules, processed sequentially rather than as a unified token sequence.
- **Architecture (w²VLA)**:
  - **Frozen backbones**: A two‑tower VLM (MetaCLIP2) for language‑aligned visual features and a Vision Foundation Model (VFM) for robust spatial/semantic patches.
  - **Input encoding**: Proprioceptive states are mapped to hidden tokens. Language instruction is parsed into separate object and skill strings, encoded by the frozen VLM.
  - **Visual modulation**: VFM patch tokens are aggregated via Attentive Feature Aggregation (AFA) and cross‑attend to hidden states, followed by causal self‑attention.
  - **Spatial conditioning (where)**: Object‑conditioned heatmaps are computed from VLM patch embeddings and the object text embedding (temperature‑scaled Softmax, ζ=0.05). These are projected to conditioning tokens and modulate hidden states via **FiLM** (Feature‑wise Linear Modulation): `h' = γ(σ) ⊙ h + β(σ)`. Residual connections with Pre‑LayerNorm preserve the injected modulation.
  - **Skill conditioning (what)**: The skill embedding is projected to a vector `σ_skill` and broadcast across the temporal dimension, applied via a second FiLM block.
  - **Action head**: An MLP predicts a chunk of future actions (delta end‑effector poses, chunk length L=10).
- **Training**: Behavior cloning loss (L1 on action deltas). To enforce decoupling, structured dropout randomly masks VFM patches with probability q=0.5, forcing the model to rely on the where/what modules rather than visual appearance.

## 3. Experiments
- **Scenarios**: Four real‑world setups, each with two (skill, object) pairs. Examples: (rotate 90°, carrot) & (place back 5cm, banana); (poke, sponge) & (nudge, toy car). 16 expert demonstrations per pair.
- **Evaluation**: Three‑point scoring per rollout (correct object, correct skill, full task completion). Each condition tested 6 times with varied object poses.
- **Baselines**:
  - **OTTER**: Frozen two‑tower VLM with text‑aware feature extraction, upgraded to same VLM (MetaCLIP2) for fairness.
  - **π0.5**: 3B PaliGemma backbone with insulated fine‑tuning (only action expert trained) to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
- **Results (Table 1)**:
  - In‑domain performance comparable across models (~95% success).
  - **Skill transfer**: w²VLA achieves **91.7%** average success; OTTER 30.6%; π0.5 38.2% (catastrophic failure, often executing the skill paired with the object during training).
- **Robustness tests**:
  - **Distractors**: Adding 3–5 novel objects drops w²VLA success by ~15% (task completion drops from 83.3% to 66.7%) but skill execution remains perfect.
  - **Unseen objects**: Replacing target objects with entirely novel ones (e.g., soda can, toothpaste) yields 86.1% skill transfer success; failures arise from geometric mismatch, not incorrect object selection.

## 4. Compute / Resources
- **Hardware**: Single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU.
- **Training**:
  - w²VLA & OTTER: 15,000 steps, batch size 32, AdamW (lr=1e-4, weight decay=1e-4, cosine decay, 200‑step warmup).
  - π0.5: 10,000 steps, batch size 8, AdamW (lr=1.5e-5, β=(0.9,0.95), weight decay=0.01, gradient clipping, cosine decay with 1,000‑step warmup).
- **Model sizes**: w²VLA – 55.17M trainable parameters; OTTER – 67.11M; π0.5 – 693.42M (frozen backbone not counted).

## 5. Experiment Breadth
- **Coverage**: Four distinct scenarios with different skill types (rotating, placing, poking, nudging). Each condition evaluated multiple times (6 rollouts per combination, varying poses). Robustness tests extend to clutter and novel objects.
- **Ablations**: Reported in Appendix A.4:
  - Visual modulation: w/o VM → poor in‑domain (66.7%) and skill transfer (52.8%); w/ VM + masking → 100% in‑domain, 94.4% transfer.
  - Module order: “where → what” yields 94.4% transfer; reversing gives 55.6%, confirming that spatial grounding must precede skill execution.
- **Fairness**: OTTER’s backbone upgraded to MetaCLIP2 to match w²VLA; π0.5’s backbone frozen to avoid overfitting in low‑data regime. However, the skill transfer evaluation is limited to two‑pair scenarios, which may underrepresent more complex real‑world variations.

## 6. Main Conclusions
- Entangling declarative and procedural knowledge is a core failure mode of modern VLAs, preventing zero‑shot skill transfer.
- w²VLA’s modular, sequential conditioning (where → what) successfully decouples these two knowledge types, enabling skill transfer with performance nearly equal to in‑domain tasks (~91.7% vs ~95%).
- The method works in low‑data regimes (16 demos per pair) and with completely unseen objects, provided geometric properties are similar.
- Even with distractors, w²VLA maintains correct skill execution; failures are primarily due to imprecise physical interactions rather than object misidentification.

## 7. Strengths
- **Architectural insight**: Explicit decoupling of declarative and procedural knowledge is principled and interpretable.
- **Efficiency**: Uses frozen, pre‑trained VLMs/VFMs, requiring only 55M trainable parameters, far fewer than π0.5’s ~693M.
- **Strong empirical results**: Zero‑shot transfer over 90% success across multiple scenarios, with thorough ablations and robustness checks.
- **Reproducibility**: Open‑sourced code and project page; detailed training details provided.

## 8. Limitations
- **Skill scope**: Only evaluated on primitive, single‑step skills (rotate, place, poke, nudge). Not tested on long‑horizon or hierarchical tasks.
- **Object geometry**: Transfer fails when geometric properties differ significantly (e.g., grasping a mug versus a bottle). The method assumes similar size/shape for successful execution.
- **Visual perturbation sensitivity**: Performance drops noticeably with distractor objects (15% decline) due to policy sensitivity to visual changes—a known IL challenge.
- **Scalability**: Only two (skill, object) pairs per scenario. The approach’s ability to scale to many skills/objects without interference is not demonstrated.
- **Real‑world hardware**: All experiments on a single SO‑101 robot arm with top‑down camera; generalizability to other embodiments is unverified.
