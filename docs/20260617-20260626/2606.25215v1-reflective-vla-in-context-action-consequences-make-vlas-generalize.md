## Abstract
Most vision-language-action (VLA) models are reactive: they predict the next action from the current instruction and observation, implicitly assuming that the current observation fully specifies the action-relevant state. In embodied control, however, embodiment-specific factors such as camera-to-robot geometry, robot calibration, or systematic actuation bias are often hard to identify from a single observation. As a result, reactive policies cannot reliably disambiguate these factors in general, overfitting to training environments and generalizing poorly at deployment. We propose Reflective VLA, which conditions each decision on a context of observation-action-consequence triplets. Each triplet records not only what the robot observed and executed, but also how the scene changed afterward, exposing the deployment-specific mapping from actions to observed effects. Architecturally, Reflective VLA routes all observation modalities through the VLM under shared attention, so the action expert reasons directly over past triplets and the current observation. A block-causal mask enables parallel multi-frame training without leakage and supports KV-cached real-time inference. On standard LIBERO and SimplerEnv-Bridge, Reflective VLA preserves strong in-distribution performance. Under distribution shift on LIBERO-Plus and the harder LIBERO-Plus-Hard, it improves average success rate by 5.4 and 4.2 percentage points over a matched reactive baseline. Ablations with a matched history-only baseline further show that action consequences -- rather than additional context length alone -- are the key to cross-environment generalization. ## 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning
- **Motivation**: Vision-language-action (VLA) models typically predict the next action reactively from the current instruction and observation alone. In embodied control, factors like camera-to-robot geometry, calibration errors, or actuation biases are **not identifiable from a single frame**, causing policies to overfit to training environments and generalize poorly at deployment.
- **Key insight**: Interaction feedback—specifically, the **observation–action–consequence triplet** (O, A, O′)—reveals how the environment responds to commands, exposing deployment-specific latent factors. The paper formulates cross-environment generalization as an **in-context learning** problem over such triplets.

## 2. Methodology
- **Core idea**: Condition each decision on a small set of past causal triplets {(Oⱼ, Aⱼ, O′ⱼ)} where O′ⱼ is the observation after executing action chunk Aⱼ. This provides evidence about the current embodiment's action-to-observation mapping.
- **Architecture**:
  - Uses a dual-system VLA: a pretrained VLM prefix (PaliGemma-3B or Qwen3-VL-2B) encodes language and multimodal observations; a continuous flow-matching action expert (hidden dimension 1024) generates action chunks.
  - All observation modalities (third-person images, wrist images, proprioception) are projected into a **shared token space**; past action chunks are projected via a learned projection gA (8 tokens per chunk).
  - A **block-causal attention mask** enables parallel supervision of K target frames in a single forward pass: each query attends only to prior completed triplets and the current observation, while its own action and consequence are masked.
  - **Training**: K ordered frames sampled per trajectory; stride randomization ([0,15] steps) prevents shortcut extrapolation from smooth actions. Loss is sum of flow-matching action losses over all K targets.
  - **Inference**: Rolling FIFO buffer of completed triplets; KV-cached reuse keeps per-step cost roughly constant.
- **Mathematical formulation**:
  - Reactive: Aₜ ~ πθ(· | L, Oₜ)
  - Reflective: Aₜ ~ πθ(· | L, H, Oₜ) with H = {(Oᵢ, Aᵢ, O′ᵢ)}^{K-1}_{i=1}

## 6. Main Conclusions and Findings
- Observation–action–consequence triplets provide a **key signal** for inferring deployment-specific factors (camera geometry, calibration bias) that are ambiguous from a single frame.
- Reflective VLA **preserves in-distribution performance** while substantially improving robustness under distribution shifts that alter the action-to-observation mapping.
- History-only context (O or O+A without O′) yields **negligible gains**, confirming that aligned action consequences are the critical ingredient.
- The method requires **no test-time fine-tuning** and adds no model parameters—only a change in how the context window is used.
- Scaling context length to 4–8 triplets captures most of the benefit; gains are largest on perturbations that directly affect sensing or actuation mapping.

## 7. Strengths
- **Novel formulation**: Casts cross-environment generalization as in-context inference over causal interaction triplets, a principled and domain-agnostic approach.
- **Efficient training and inference**: Block-causal mask enables parallel multi-frame training (O(K) → O(1) per sequence); KV-caching keeps inference cost sub-linear in context length.
- **Strong empirical design**: Ablations isolate the contribution of action consequences, and the diagnostic LIBERO-Plus-Hard benchmark directly tests the hypothesized mechanism.
- **Real-world validation**: Demonstrates practical gains in cross-camera generalization on physical hardware.

## 8. Limitations
- **Context length capped at K=8** due to GPU memory during block-causal training; longer contexts might yield further gains.
- **First action reactive**: The model cannot condition on any triplet before the first executed chunk, limiting initial-step adaptation.
- **Assumes observable consequences within chunk horizon**: Delayed effects or contact-rich dynamics may not be captured, requiring longer horizons.
- **Potential shortcut bias**: Adjacent action chunks are temporally smooth; stride randomization partially mitigates but does not eliminate the risk of the model extrapolating from past actions rather than reasoning from consequences.
- **Real-world evaluation limited**: Only two tabletop tasks with 25 trials per condition; broader tasks and embodiments are needed to assess generality.
- **Compute resources**: Training requires 8 H20 GPUs; accessibility for smaller labs may be limited.
