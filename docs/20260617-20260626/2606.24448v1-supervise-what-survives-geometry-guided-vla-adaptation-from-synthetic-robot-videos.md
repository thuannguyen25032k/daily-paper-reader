## Abstract
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models require large-scale video-action pairs, yet real teleoperation remains scarce. While generated robot videos offer a scalable alternative, existing methods treat them as real robot data by recovering pseudo-actions from synthesized pixels. We argue that deriving low-level control from generated visuals is a mismatched abstraction. A video captures only \emph{geometry}: the spatial trajectory representing the \emph{where} of a task. A real demonstration captures \emph{control}: the exact motor commands representing the \emph{how}. Human-to-robot video generation preserves these unequally: the visible geometry survives the generation process, while the underlying control signals are lost. This \textbf{Asymmetric Preservation Principle} dictates a clean rule: this surviving geometry should solely supervise visual perception, leaving control to real demonstrations. Following this principle, we propose \textbf{GRA} (\textbf{G}eometry-guided \textbf{R}epresentation \textbf{A}lignment), which extracts the geometric content as future 2D end-effector waypoints, computed from the source human video through pose estimation, retargeting, simulation, and calibrated projection, and routes them to the VLA vision backbone via an auxiliary 2D head. The action head is trained on real demonstrations only. During fine-tuning, the waypoint loss persists as a \textbf{spatial representation anchor} that prevents the backbone from losing its geometric grounding. On real-robot tasks, GRA outperforms pseudo-action baselines under matched data budgets and narrows the gap to policies trained with substantially more real demonstrations, suggesting that correctly routed geometry bridges generated videos to robot policies more reliably than recovered actions.

### 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning
- **Motivation:** Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models require large-scale video-action pairs for training. Real teleoperation is scarce due to hardware and operator costs. Human-to-robot video generation offers scalable synthetic data but lacks action labels.
- **Problem:** Existing methods recover pseudo-actions from generated videos (e.g., inverse dynamics or retargeting), but this creates a mismatch: video generation preserves only **geometry** (spatial trajectory, "where") while losing **control** signals (motor commands, "how"). Supervising the action head with distorted pseudo-actions degrades policy performance.
- **Core Insight:** The **Asymmetric Preservation Principle** states that generated videos reliably preserve spatial structure but not control dynamics. Therefore, synthetic videos should supervise only the visual perception (geometry), while control must be grounded in real demonstrations.

### 2. Methodology: GRA (Geometry-guided Representation Alignment)
- **Key Idea:** Route geometric supervision (future 2D end-effector waypoints) to the VLA vision backbone via an auxiliary head. The action head trains exclusively on real robot data.
- **Waypoint Derivation:** From source human video (not generated pixels) via pipeline:  
  `Hand detection (Grounding DINO) → Segmentation (SAM2) → 3D hand pose (HaMeR) → Retargeting to robot frame → Physics simulation (MuJoCo) → Calibrated camera projection`  
  Result: target waypoints \( y_t = (p_{t+1}, \dots, p_{t+K}) \in \mathbb{R}^{K\times 2} \).
- **Two-Stage Training:**
  - **Stage 1 – Geometric Pretraining:** Train vision backbone \(\phi_v\) and auxiliary 2D head \(h_{2D}\) on synthetic frames \((I_s^t, y_s^t)\) with L2 waypoint loss \(\mathcal{L}_{2D}^s\). Language model and action head are not used.
  - **Stage 2 – Action Fine-tuning with Spatial Anchor:** Add action head \(\phi_a\) and fine-tune on real demonstrations \((I_r^t, l, \Delta a_t, y_r^t)\). Total loss:  
    \(\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{act} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{2D}^r\)  
    where \(\mathcal{L}_{act}\) is L1 delta-action regression, and \(\mathcal{L}_{2D}^r\) is the same waypoint loss on real frames (requires no extra labels, as \(y_r^t\) comes from recorded proprioception). The persistent waypoint loss anchors spatial structure, preventing drift from action gradients.

### 6. Main Conclusions and Findings
