## Abstract
Evaluating generalist robot manipulation policies in the real world is expensive, slow, and difficult to scale. Action-conditioned video world models offer a scalable alternative by simulating policy rollouts. Autoregressive rollouts accumulate compounding errors, observations across multiple camera views must remain mutually consistent, and the evaluator must generalize to policies whose behaviors lie outside the training distribution. We address these challenges with SC3-Eval, a self-consistent video generation recipe that adapts a pre-trained video foundation model into an accurate policy evaluator by enforcing three complementary forms of consistency. First, forward-inverse dynamics consistency jointly trains the model to predict frames from actions and to recover actions from frames, anchoring generated rollouts to a physically plausible action manifold and counteracting the drift a forward-only model cannot penalize. Second, cross-view consistency trains the model to inpaint each camera view from the other, keeping the multi-camera observation coherent over long rollouts without any explicit memory mechanism. Third, test-time consistency reuses the inverse dynamics mode at inference as a per-action-chunk uncertainty signal that terminates rollouts whose generated frames drift away from the requested actions. We also demonstrate SC3-Eval rollouts reproduce the failure modes that policies exhibit in real-world rollouts, supporting fine-grained diagnostic comparison rather than aggregate ranking alone. Across seven real-world vision-language-action policies, SC3-Eval attains a closed-loop Pearson correlation of $0.929$ and MMRV of $0.119$, outperforming three strong prior video-model-based baselines, and generalizes to new tasks.

### 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning
- **Motivation**: Real-world evaluation of generalist robot manipulation policies is prohibitively expensive, slow, and hard to scale due to physical robot time, environment resets, and human supervision. Action-conditioned video world models offer a scalable alternative by simulating policy rollouts, but they face three key challenges: (i) autoregressive drift as small errors compound over long horizons, (ii) multi-camera observations becoming mutually inconsistent, and (iii) generalization to policies whose behaviors lie outside the training distribution.
- **Goal**: Develop a video-based policy evaluator that faithfully predicts real-world performance, preserves ranking of policy checkpoints, and reproduces specific failure modes – not just aggregate success rates.

### 2. Methodology: Core Idea and Technical Details
- **Core Idea**: Adapt a pre-trained video foundation model (Cosmos3-Nano) into a closed-loop policy evaluator by enforcing three complementary forms of consistency:
  - **Forward-Inverse Dynamics Consistency**: Jointly train the model to predict frames from actions (forward) and to recover actions from frames (inverse). Parameter sharing between the two modes anchors generated rollouts to a physically plausible action manifold and penalizes drift that a forward-only model cannot detect.
  - **Cross-View Consistency**: Train a cross-view inpainting mode that predicts missing camera views from the remaining view and actions, keeping multi-camera observations coherent over long rollouts without an explicit memory buffer.
  - **Test-Time Consistency**: At inference, reuse the inverse dynamics mode to compute a per-chunk consistency error \( U_{\text{chunk}}(t) = \frac{1}{l} \sum_{i=t}^{t+l-1} \| a_i - \hat{a}_i \|_2 \) (difference between commanded actions and actions recovered from generated frames). Terminate the rollout when \( U_{\text{chunk}}(t) > \tau \) to avoid compounding drift.
- **Training**: Use a unified transformer with three modes (forward dynamics, cross-view inpainting, inverse dynamics) sampled randomly per mini-batch. All modes share parameters and are trained with a flow-matching objective. Prediction horizon \( l' = 24 \) frames is longer than the execution horizon \( l = 16 \) frames (prediction-execution horizon decoupling) to better leverage the pre-training prior and provide richer supervision.
- **Inference**: Closed-loop rollout with early termination based on the inverse-dynamics error. The threshold \( \tau = 0.02 \) is tuned on a held-out set.

### 6. Main Conclusions and Findings
- Self-consistent video generation (SC3-Eval) enables faithful, scalable policy evaluation, achieving high correlation with real-world performance and preserving policy rankings (best MMRV).
- The unified forward-inverse dynamics model implicitly grounds rollouts, reducing drift. Cross-view inpainting maintains multi-camera coherence. Early termination via inverse-dynamics error improves closed-loop reliability.
- SC3-Eval generalizes to an OOD task semantic and reproduces specific failure modes, supporting diagnostic analysis beyond simple success-rate prediction.

### 7. Strengths
- **Novel consistency framework**: Joint forward-inverse dynamics as an implicit anti-drift regularizer; cross-view inpainting for multi-view coherence; uncertainty-driven early termination from the same model without extra supervision.
- **Strong empirical results**: Outperforms three strong baselines across all metrics in both in-distribution and out-of-distribution settings.
- **Diagnostic capability**: Reproduces per-trajectory failure modes, enabling fine-grained policy debugging.
- **Practical setup**: Trained on large real-world dataset, evaluated on multiple VLA policy checkpoints with realistic tasks.

### 8. Limitations
- **Inference speed**: 2.3 seconds per chunk on a single GB200 GPU – orders of magnitude slower than physics-based simulators, limiting use for routine checkpoint selection during training.
- **Short-horizon manipulation**: Tested on episodes of ~20 seconds; longer rollouts may suffer from compounding local drift and visual degradation beyond the pre-trained backbone’s temporal coherence prior.
- **Single-task domain**: Trained and evaluated only on table-bussing (one workspace, fixed object set); generalization to diverse tasks and environments is not demonstrated.
- **No multi-task evaluation**: The evaluator is trained only on the forward-bussing task; the OOD task is a semantic mapping shift but shares the same visual and motion statistics. True domain shift (e.g., different objects, backgrounds) remains untested.
- **Dependency on human annotation**: Success rates and failure modes are annotated by humans; automatic annotation with VLMs shows promise but is not yet fully reliable.
