## Abstract
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models provide a unified paradigm for robotic manipulation, yet their real-world deployment is often bottlenecked by execution efficiency. While existing efforts predominantly focus on compute-centric efficiency to reduce per-step inference latency, the intrinsic \textbf{policy efficiency} of these models remains largely unexplored. Policy efficiency is fundamentally affected by two factors, namely the effective executable length of predicted action chunks and the total physical steps required to complete a task. These two factors jointly determine the total number of forward inference calls during execution. We observe that current VLA policies struggle with planning unreliability and action redundancy, suffering from severe prediction degradation at the tail of action chunks and tending to generate unnecessarily redundant physical steps. To address this, we propose \textbf{PolicyTrim}, a reinforcement learning-based post-training framework that extends the reliable action chunk length and reduces redundant physical steps. For reliable chunk extension, we employ a dynamic exploration strategy that explicitly rewards the successful completion of longer executable lengths, progressively pushing the trustworthy prediction horizon to its empirical limit. For step efficiency, we design a redundancy-aware reward that directly favors successful task completions with fewer steps while penalizing unreproducible shortcuts, effectively eliminating redundant physical actions. Extensive experiments across three benchmarks and three VLA models demonstrate that PolicyTrim improves action chunk utilization by 3$\times$ and reduces physical execution steps by 51.4\%. Ultimately, our framework delivers up to a 5.83$\times$ end-to-end deployment speedup without compromising task success rates.

# PolicyTrim: Summary

## 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models are powerful for robotic manipulation but suffer from **intrinsic policy inefficiency** during deployment, independent of per-step compute latency. Two key issues degrade efficiency:
- **Unreliable tail predictions** in action chunks: prediction quality degrades toward the end of each predicted action sequence, forcing conservative truncation (short execution windows) and increasing inference calls.
- **Action redundancy**: policies generate unnecessarily many physical steps, often due to corrective motions caused by accumulated errors.

Together, these factors inflate the total number of forward inference calls per task, bottlenecking real-world speed. Existing efforts focus on per-step acceleration (e.g., token pruning, quantization, KV-cache reuse) but ignore policy efficiency. **PolicyTrim** aims to boost intrinsic policy efficiency via RL-based post-training without architectural changes.

## 2. Methodology

**Core idea**: Two-stage reinforcement learning (using Group Relative Policy Optimization, GRPO) that sequentially (1) extends the reliable action chunk length, and (2) reduces redundant physical steps.

- **Stage 1 – Reliable Action Chunk Extension**:
  - Within each GRPO group, assign different acceptance ratios $\gamma_i$ (fraction of max chunk length $H$) to each trajectory.
  - Reward: $R_{\text{ext}}(\tau_i) = \mathbb{I}_{\text{succ}}^{(i)} \cdot (R_{\text{succ}} + \beta \cdot \gamma_i)$; activation only if at least one trajectory succeeds.
  - Dynamic exploration probes different horizons in parallel, pushing the reliable frontier via higher rewards for longer successful execution.

- **Stage 2 – Redundancy-Aware Step Reduction**:
  - Step-saving reward: $R_{\text{step}}(\tau_i) = \max(0, \frac{S_{\text{base}} - S_i}{S_{\text{base}}})$ where $S_{\text{base}}$ is a budget (~1.3× baseline average).
  - **Group-anchored stability penalty** prevents shortcuts: $P_{\text{stab}}(\tau_i) = \lambda_{\text{stab}} \cdot \tanh\left(\frac{|S_i - \mu_{\text{group}}|}{\max(\sigma_{\text{group}}, \sigma_{\text{floor}})}\right)$.
  - Final reward: $R_{\text{eff}}(\tau_i) = \mathbb{I}_{\text{succ}}^{(i)} \cdot (R_{\text{succ}} + R_{\text{step}} - P_{\text{stab}})$.
  - Advantages computed within group, then GRPO update with KL penalty to reference policy.

## 7. Strengths

- **Novel problem formulation**: Clearly separates intrinsic policy efficiency from compute efficiency, addressing a critical deployment bottleneck.
- **Practical method**: No architectural changes, no need for extra demonstrations; compatible with existing compute acceleration techniques.
- **Robust regularization**: Group-anchored stability penalty prevents shortcut exploitation, maintaining high success while reducing steps.

## 8. Limitations

- **Architecture dependence**: Stage 1 (chunk extension) not applicable to OpenVLA-OFT due to fixed parallel decoding; only Stage 2 applied. For autoregressive OpenVLA, Stage 1 also not used. Method is most effective for diffusion-based models like π0.5 and GR00T.
- **Task scope**: All tasks are **tabletop manipulation** (pick-and-place, stacking, etc.). Generalization to mobile manipulation, locomotion, or dynamic tasks not evaluated.
- **Sim-to-real gap**: Real-world results rely on simulation-based RL training followed by SFT on a few real demos. The robustness under large domain shifts is not thoroughly tested beyond two visual perturbations.
- **Training cost**: Requires ~68 hours on 8 H100 GPUs, which may be prohibitive for low-resource settings, though it's a one-time cost.
- **Potential bias**: The step-saving reward and budget $S_{\text{base}}$ are based on baseline statistics; setting may favor tasks where baseline step count variance is low. The stability penalty uses hyper-parameters ($\sigma_{\text{floor}}$, $\lambda_{\text{stab}}$) that may need tuning per domain.
- **Safety**: No explicit discussion of failure modes under aggressive step reduction (e.g., collision risks). The group-anchored penalty mitigates some but not all risks; deployment in safety-critical settings requires additional verification.
