## Abstract
Robot learning has advanced rapidly in learning control, but learning the physical body of a robot remains much more difficult because jointly searching over design and control creates a very large combinatorial problem. Here, we present a data-driven framework for generating robot hands from human demonstrations. Instead of learning a complex controller together with each candidate design, we generate robot hand designs using the same simple control policy used after fabrication: matching fingertip positions through inverse kinematics. Using more than 4 million frames of human fingertip motion from everyday manipulation, our algorithm optimizes tree-structured robot hands to reproduce desired target motions. The framework produced both a 6-degree-of-freedom (DoF) general-purpose hand and lower-DoF task-specific hands with spatial four-bar mimic joints. To accelerate the search over designs, we trained a reinforcement-learning (RL) actor to propose good hand designs and joint angles, reducing search time from hours to minutes. We fabricated the mechanisms directly as one-piece articulated structures with print-in-place joints. In real-world experiments, the 6-DoF hand achieved highly accurate teleoperated fingertip tracking better than available commercial robot hands, whereas the specialized 3-DoF hands reproduced structured human and synthetic trajectories with reduced mechanical complexity. These results showed that large-scale human motion data can be used not only to train robot controllers but also as a reference for optimizing and generating the physical embodiment of robots.

## 1. Core Problem and Overall Meaning
Robot learning has focused on control, but designing the physical body (embodiment) is difficult because hardware and control must be co-optimized, creating a large combinatorial search space. The paper proposes a **data-driven framework** that uses large-scale human fingertip motion data (over 4 million frames) to **generate robot hand designs** directly, without complex controller learning. The key insight: use the same simple inverse kinematics (IK) controller during design optimization as during deployment, so the resulting hardware is inherently suited to the intended control policy.

## 2. Methodology
- **Core idea**: Optimize hand kinematics (link lengths, joint orientations, mimic joint parameters) to minimize fingertip tracking error relative to human demonstration trajectories under IK control.  
- **Design space**: Tree-structured two-finger hands, either fully actuated or with low-DoF spatial four-bar mimic joints (Bennett linkages) for passive coupling.  
- **Loss function**:  
  \[
  \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{track}} + \lambda_{\text{joint}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{joint}} + \lambda_{\text{design}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{design}} + \lambda_{\text{col}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{col}}
  \]  
  - \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{track}}\): L1 distance between predicted and target fingertip positions.  
  - \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{joint}}\): temporal smoothness of joint angles.  
  - \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{design}}\): regularization on link lengths and mimic parameters.  
  - \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{col}}\): self-collision penalty via line-segment distances.  
- **Optimization flow**:  
  1. Fully actuated hands: differentiable gradient descent (GD) over both design and joint angles.  
  2. Low-DoF mimic hands: GD becomes highly nonconvex due to Bennett closure constraints → use a **trajectory-conditioned actor** (RL) that learns to propose good initializations (design + joint angles).  
  3. Actor: a 3-layer MLP that outputs mean action; samples candidates, evaluates via GD refinement, and updates toward best candidate (reward = product of sigmoid-normalized costs).  
- **Fabrication**: Optimized designs are converted to meshes (boxes, cylinders, rings) and 3D-printed as one-piece articulated structures with print-in-place joints.
