## Abstract
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have emerged as a powerful paradigm for generalist robot manipulation, yet they lack geometric inductive biases: policies trained at specific orientations require substantially more data to generalize across rotational configurations. We present \textsc{EquiVLA}, the first general framework for end-to-end $\mathrm{SO}(2)$-equivariant VLA models, applicable to any architecture coupling a frozen vision-language backbone with a flow-matching Diffusion Transformer action head. \textsc{EquiVLA} introduces \textsc{EquiPerceptor}, which produces approximately $\mathrm{SO}(2)$-equivariant visual representations from frozen ViT features; and \textsc{EquiActor}, an exactly $\mathrm{SO}(2)$-equivariant flow-matching Diffusion Transformer action head. Together, they establish an approximate $\mathrm{SO}(2)$ equivariance chain from camera observations to predicted action sequences. Instantiated on GR00T~N1.5 and evaluated across four LIBERO suites, CALVIN ABCD$\to$D, and five real-robot tasks on Mobile ALOHA, \textsc{EquiVLA} achieves $92.6\%$ average success on LIBERO (vs. $78.1\%$ baseline), an average sequence length of $4.03$ on CALVIN (vs. $3.45$), and improves real-robot success from $54\%$ to $72\%$.

### Methodology: Core Idea and Technical Details

- **Overall architecture:** EquiVLA comprises two composable modules applied to a base VLA (e.g., GR00T N1.5):

    - **EquiActor:** An exactly SO(2)-equivariant flow-matching Diffusion Transformer action head.
        - All linear projections, attention Q/K/V, state/action encoders, and action decoder are replaced by G-steerable layers in regular feature space.
        - Attention scores use geometric inner product (invariant), yielding equivariant outputs.
        - Action representation: end-effector position/orientation transformed according to irreducible representations of SO(2) (Equation 4 in paper).
    - **End-to-end equivariance chain:** EquiPerceptor provides approximately equivariant visual context to EquiActor, which then produces exactly equivariant action sequences. The overall equivariance error is bounded by the product of the ViT’s feature norm, the Lipschitz constant of EquiActor, and the representation defect \(\Delta\) from approximate homomorphism.

### Main Conclusions and Findings

- EquiVLA demonstrates that rotational equivariance can be effectively composed onto existing pretrained VLA models without modifying the frozen VLM.
- The modular approach (EquiPerceptor + EquiActor) yields significant improvements in both simulation and real-world benchmarks, with consistent gains across all tasks.
- The geometric inductive bias provides better data efficiency: EquiVLA outperforms the baseline even at 10% demonstrations, and the gap widens at larger data scales.
- Equivariance error is substantially reduced (27.3×), confirming architectural enforcement rather than mere data augmentation.
- Trade-offs: C8 provides the best accuracy-latency balance; larger groups (C16) offer marginal gains at higher cost.
- Benefits are largest for tasks requiring orientation-invariant manipulation (e.g., Letter Aligning: +30 pp); tasks with little rotational variation (Shorts Folding) see no degradation.

### Strengths

- **Modular design:** EquiVLA can be applied to any VLA that couples a frozen VLM with a flow-matching DiT head, without modifying pretrained weights.
- **Theoretical grounding:** Provides formal bounds on equivariance error and sample complexity reduction.
- **Comprehensive evaluation:** Covers multiple simulation benchmarks and real-robot tasks with thorough ablations (group size, data budget, orientation shifts).
- **Practical improvements:** Achieves large gains on real-robot tasks with only 150 demos per task, demonstrating practical viability.
- **Handles approximation from frozen ViT:** The token-level FA with nearest-neighbor reassignment is a pragmatic solution that maintains acceptable equivariance.

### Limitations

- **Symmetry scope:** Only SO(2) (planar rotation about gravity axis). Tasks with out-of-plane rotations (e.g., wall-mounted manipulation) require extension to SO(3) or SE(3).
- **Computational cost:** Token-level FA requires |G| additional ViT passes (8 for C8), increasing latency to ~194 ms/step (vs. 64 ms for baseline). This may be prohibitive for high-frequency control.
- **Action head trained from scratch:** EquiActor cannot reuse pretrained DiT weights due to steerable layer constraints; it requires full training, which may limit scalability to very large VLA models.
- **Approximate equivariance:** The frozen ViT’s discretization introduces a non-zero representation defect, especially for non-C4 rotations (45° offsets). Equivariance is exact only when the ViT’s patch grid is aligned with the rotation subgroup.
- **Limited real-robot validation:** Only 5 tasks, all tabletop with top-down camera; no deployment on dynamic or cluttered environments.
- **No temporal history** in CALVIN experiments due to cost – potential loss of multi-frame context benefits.
